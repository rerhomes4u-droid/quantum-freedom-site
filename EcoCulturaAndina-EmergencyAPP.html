Cardiac Emergency & CPR Response

Step-by-step instructions to perform CPR and use an AED when someone is unresponsive and not breathing

1

Check for Responsiveness

Tap the person's shoulder and shout 'Are you okay?' Look for any movement or response.

If no response:

Call emergency services immediately or have someone else call while you begin CPR.

2

Check Breathing

Look for chest movement, listen for breathing sounds, and feel for breath on your cheek for no more than 10 seconds.

If not breathing or only gasping:

Begin CPR immediately. Gasping is not normal breathing!

3

Begin Chest Compressions

Place the heel of one hand on the center of the chest, place your other hand on top, and interlock fingers.

4

Use AED if Available

Turn on the AED and follow the voice prompts. Apply pads to bare chest as shown in diagrams.

Important:

Do not touch the person while the AED is analyzing or delivering a shock. Continue CPR until emergency services arrive.

Choking Emergency Response

How to perform the Heimlich maneuver for adults, children, and infants

1

Recognize Choking

Look for these signs: inability to speak, difficulty breathing, coughing weakly, clutching throat, skin turning blue.

Ask:

'Are you choking?' If they can speak or cough effectively, encourage them to keep coughing.

2

Abdominal Thrusts (Heimlich)

For adults and children over 1 year:

  • Stand behind the person and wrap your arms around their waist
  • Make a fist with one hand and place it just above their navel
  • Grab your fist with your other hand and give quick upward thrusts
3

For Infants Under 1 Year

Never perform abdominal thrusts on infants. Instead:

  • Place baby face down on your forearm, supporting head and jaw
  • Give 5 back blows between shoulder blades with heel of hand
  • If object doesn't dislodge, turn baby over and give 5 chest thrusts
4

If Person Becomes Unresponsive

If the choking person loses consciousness:

  • Carefully lower them to the ground
  • Begin CPR starting with chest compressions
  • Check mouth for object before giving breaths

Severe Allergic Reaction Response

How to recognize and treat anaphylaxis - a life-threatening allergic emergency

1

Recognize Anaphylaxis Symptoms

Look for these signs developing rapidly (within minutes to hours):

  • Difficulty breathing, wheezing, or tightness in throat
  • Swelling of lips, tongue, or face
  • Hives, itching, or skin redness
  • Rapid heartbeat, dizziness, or fainting
  • Nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain
2

Use Epinephrine Auto-Injector

If available and prescribed to the person:

1
Remove safety cap
2
Press firmly against outer thigh
3
Hold for 3-10 seconds
Important:

Epinephrine can be injected through clothing. Do not inject into buttocks or other areas. Massage injection area for 10 seconds after.

3

Emergency Positioning & Monitoring

After administering epinephrine:

  • Have person lie flat on their back with feet elevated
  • If breathing is difficult, help them sit up
  • If vomiting, turn them on their side
  • Loosen tight clothing and cover with blanket
  • Do not give anything to drink
4

Second Dose & Ongoing Care

If symptoms persist or return:

  • Second epinephrine dose may be given after 5-15 minutes if needed
  • Continue monitoring breathing and consciousness
  • Be prepared to perform CPR if breathing stops
  • Even if symptoms improve, medical evaluation is essential

Severe Bleeding Control

How to stop life-threatening bleeding using direct pressure, wound packing, and tourniquets

1

Ensure Safety & Expose Wound

Before approaching:

  • Ensure the scene is safe for you to help
  • Wear gloves if available to protect from bloodborne pathogens
  • Remove or cut away clothing to clearly see the wound
  • Look for the source of bleeding - don't be distracted by less serious injuries
2

Apply Direct Pressure

Technique:

3

Use Tourniquet for Limb Bleeding

For severe bleeding from arms or legs that doesn't stop with direct pressure:

  • Place tourniquet 2-3 inches above wound (closer to torso)
  • Pull strap tight and secure fastener
  • Twist rod until bleeding stops
  • Secure rod in place and note time applied
Important:

Once applied, do not remove tourniquet. Medical professionals will remove it in controlled setting. Tourniquets hurt but save lives!

4

Wound Packing & Ongoing Care

For deep wounds where direct pressure isn't effective:

  • Pack wound tightly with gauze or clean cloth
  • Apply direct pressure over packed wound
  • Keep person warm and monitor for shock
  • Do not give food or water
  • Continue care until medical help arrives

Hypothermia & Altitude Sickness Response

How to recognize and treat cold exposure and altitude-related emergencies in mountain environments

1

Recognize Hypothermia

Look for these signs of dangerously low body temperature:

  • Shivering (may stop in severe cases)
  • Confusion, drowsiness, or slurred speech
  • Slow, shallow breathing
  • Weak pulse, loss of coordination
  • Cold, pale skin
2

Treat Hypothermia

For mild to moderate hypothermia:

  • Move to warm, dry shelter
  • Remove wet clothing
  • Warm center of body first (chest, neck, groin)
  • Use skin-to-skin contact under loose layers
  • Give warm, sweet drinks if person is alert
Do Not:

Do not rub or massage extremities. Do not use direct heat sources. Do not give alcohol. Handle gently to avoid cardiac arrest.

3

Recognize Altitude Sickness

Symptoms of acute mountain sickness (AMS):

  • Headache (usually throbbing, worse at night)
  • Nausea, vomiting, loss of appetite
  • Fatigue, weakness, dizziness
  • Difficulty sleeping
Severe Symptoms (HAPE/HACE):

Cough with pink frothy sputum, confusion, loss of coordination, severe shortness of breath at rest - these require immediate descent!

4

Treat Altitude Sickness

For mild AMS:

  • Stop ascent and rest at current altitude
  • Drink plenty of fluids
  • Take pain relievers for headache
  • Consider descent if symptoms don't improve in 24 hours

For severe AMS (HAPE/HACE):

  • Immediate descent is the primary treatment
  • Use oxygen if available
  • Medications may help but don't replace descent
  • Seek medical evacuation immediately

Snake & Insect Bite Response

First aid for venomous bites and stings encountered in wilderness areas

1

Snake Bite First Aid

For venomous snake bites:

  • Move away from the snake to avoid additional bites
  • Keep the person calm and still
  • Position bite below heart level if possible
  • Remove tight clothing or jewelry near bite
  • Note snake appearance for identification
Do Not:

Do not cut the wound, suck out venom, use tourniquet, apply ice, or give alcohol/caffeine. These can cause more harm!

2

Spider & Scorpion Stings

For venomous spider and scorpion stings:

  • Wash area with soap and water
  • Apply cool compress to reduce pain and swelling
  • Elevate affected limb if possible
  • Seek medical attention for severe reactions
Severe Symptoms:

Difficulty breathing, muscle spasms, severe pain, nausea/vomiting, sweating - seek immediate medical care!

3

Bee & Wasp Stings

For bee and wasp stings:

  • Remove stinger by scraping with credit card or fingernail
  • Wash area with soap and water
  • Apply cold pack to reduce swelling
  • Take antihistamine for itching if available
Allergic Reaction:

If signs of severe allergic reaction develop (difficulty breathing, swelling, dizziness), use epinephrine auto-injector if available and seek emergency care immediately.

4

Tick Bites & Prevention

For tick bites:

  • Use fine-tipped tweezers to grasp tick close to skin
  • Pull upward with steady, even pressure
  • Clean bite area and hands thoroughly
  • Save tick in container for identification if possible
Watch for Symptoms:

Fever, rash, headache, fatigue, muscle/joint pain in weeks following bite may indicate tick-borne illness. Seek medical attention.

Fractures & Sprains Response

Immobilization techniques and pain management for musculoskeletal injuries

1

Recognize Fractures vs Sprains

Signs of possible fracture:

  • Visible deformity or abnormal positioning
  • Bone protruding through skin (open fracture)
  • Heard or felt a snap or grind at time of injury
  • Severe pain that worsens with movement
  • Inability to bear weight or use limb

Signs of sprain:

  • Pain, swelling, and bruising around joint
  • Limited ability to move joint
  • Feeling of looseness in joint
2

Initial Care for Suspected Fracture

For suspected fractures:

  • Keep injured area still - don't try to realigen bone
  • Stop any bleeding with direct pressure
  • Apply ice pack wrapped in cloth to reduce swelling
  • Treat for shock if signs present
For Open Fractures:

Cover wound with sterile dressing but do not attempt to push bone back under skin. Control bleeding with direct pressure around (not on) protruding bone.
